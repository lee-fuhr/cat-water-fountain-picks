How to choose cat fountains

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Choose a cat water fountain by focusing on three things: material that resists bacteria (stainless steel or ceramic), a quiet pump that won’t spook your cat, and easy access for cleaning. A good fountain encourages hydration, but only if it’s simple to maintain and free of hidden crevices.

Why choose a fountain instead of a bowl?

Cats evolved to prefer moving water, in the wild, still water can harbor bacteria, so a flowing stream signals freshness. A fountain mimics that instinct, often encouraging picky drinkers to lap more throughout the day. Beyond hydration, the constant circulation keeps the water oxygenated and less prone to stagnation. Many owners report their cats drink noticeably more when a fountain replaces a bowl, which can help prevent urinary and kidney issues over time.

Plastic, stainless steel, or ceramic: which material is best?

Plastic fountains are the most budget-friendly and light, but they can scratch easily. Those micro-scratches trap bacteria and are hard to fully sanitize. Some cats also develop chin acne from plastic, so it’s not the top pick for long-term health. Stainless steel and ceramic are the two gold standards. Stainless steel is nearly indestructible, dishwasher-safe, and non-porous. Ceramic offers a heavier, tip-resistant design and comes in many dish-friendly finishes. Both resist bacterial growth better than plastic. The trade-off is weight, ceramic can chip if dropped, and stainless steel models sometimes have fewer aesthetic options.

What should you look for in pump quality and noise?

A whisper-quiet pump is the difference between a fountain your cat uses and one it ignores. Look for models described as “ultra-quiet” or with a submersible pump that sits low in the water. Pumps with ceramic bearings or magnetic drive tend to run smoother and last longer than cheap plastic impellers. Even a quiet pump may hum if the water level gets low, so choose a fountain with a generous reservoir that won’t run dry overnight. Many good fountains also let you adjust the flow rate, which helps if your cat prefers a gentle trickle over a strong stream.

How often should you clean a cat water fountain?

Plan on a full disassembly and scrub every three to seven days, depending on your household’s dust and fur. Water residue, slime, and cat kibble crumbs build up fast in any moving-water system, and a dirty fountain defeats the purpose. Models with wide openings and few nooks are far easier to clean than those with intricate spouts or hidden chambers. Look for a fountain that fits in your dishwasher (top rack) or can be hand-washed with a simple bottle brush. The pump itself needs monthly maintenance, soaking in dilute vinegar can dissolve mineral scale and keep it running.

Do filters matter, and which type is right?

Most fountains use a multi-stage filter: a foam or mesh pre-filter catches hair and debris, an activated carbon layer removes odors and chlorine taste, and sometimes an ion-exchange resin softens hard water. These filters improve palatability but won’t remove bacteria, that’s why cleaning the fountain regularly is still essential. Replace filters roughly every four weeks, or sooner if you notice a musty smell or reduced flow. Some owners skip the carbon filters and rely on frequent water changes to cut recurring costs. If you do that, be prepared to clean the fountain more often to keep biofilm under control.

Frequently asked questions

How often do I need to change the fountain filter?

Most manufacturers recommend swapping the filter every four weeks. If your water is very hard or you have multiple cats, you might need to replace it every two to three weeks to keep the water tasting fresh.

Can I use tap water in a cat water fountain?

Yes, tap water works fine for most cats. If your tap water has a strong chlorine taste or high mineral content, the carbon filter in the fountain will help, consider using filtered tap water if your cat still seems hesitant.

Is it safe to leave the fountain running all the time?

Yes, these fountains are designed for continuous operation. Just make sure the reservoir never runs dry, a dry pump can overheat and fail. Most models have a low-water indicator or let you see the water level at a glance.

My cat is scared of the fountain. What can I do?

Start by placing the fountain near the cat’s existing water bowl, turned off for a few days. Then plug it in during supervised time. Some cats prefer a very low flow, look for a fountain with adjustable speed or a gentle drinking surface. You can also put a familiar bowl beneath the stream until your cat gets used to the movement.

Do fountains really get cats to drink more water?

Many anecdotal reports and some small studies suggest they do, because cats are naturally attracted to moving water. Even if your cat only drinks a little more, the increased hydration can be beneficial for kidney and urinary tract health. It’s not a cure-all, but it’s a popular and low-risk tool.

How do I clean the pump without damaging it?

Most pumps disassemble easily by pulling off the cover and lifting out the impeller. Soak the parts in a mixture of white vinegar and warm water for fifteen to thirty minutes to dissolve mineral scale, then rinse well. Never use soap on the pump, residue can harm your cat. Check the manufacturer’s instructions for your specific model.

In shortA good cat water fountain is a simple way to support your cat’s everyday health, but only if you pick one that fits your cleaning habits and your cat’s personality. Focus on material, pump noise, and ease of maintenance, then let your cat’s enthusiasm (or hesitation) guide your final choice.
